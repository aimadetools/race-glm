## Thread 2: SAFE Notes

1/7 Your SAFE note has a cap AND a discount.

Which one actually matters at conversion?

It's the LOWER valuation.

Example: You have a $5M cap and 20% discount. Company raises at $6M.

With cap: Conversion price = $5M / post-money
With discount: Conversion price = $6M × 0.8 / post-money

The discount wins you a better deal.

But wait — what if you have TWO SAFEs with different terms?

One has $5M cap, other has MFN.

The MFN SAFE gets the BEST conversion price from all other SAFEs.

So it benefits from the capped one but gets MFN protection too.

## Thread 4: Unit Economics

1/8 What's your SaaS LTV:CAC ratio?

If you can't answer instantly, you're in trouble.

Because this is THE metric investors care about.

LTV:CAC = How much a customer is worth ÷ How much it costs to acquire them

Here's what healthy looks like by stage:

Pre-seed: 1.5:1 — you're finding product-market fit
Seed: 2.5:1 — showing early traction
Series A: 3:1 — sustainable unit economics
Series B+: 4:1 — efficient growth engine

Below 2:1? You're losing money on every customer.

Above 5:1? You're underinvesting in growth — could be growing faster.

## Thread 5: Startup Offer Red Flag Scanner

1/7 Just got a startup offer letter?

Don't sign until you read this.

Most equity offers have hidden terms that silently wipe out 10-30% of your equity.

Here's what to look for:

🚩 Red Flag #1: "Double-trigger" vesting acceleration

This means you only get accelerated vesting if BOTH:
1. You're fired without cause
2. There's an acquisition

Most startups only get acquired once.
If you're fired before the exit → you get nothing.

Single-trigger acceleration is standard at top startups.
Double-trigger is a silent pay cut.

---

🚩 Red Flag #2: "Unapproved" exercise window

Standard: You have 90 days post-termination to exercise options.

But some offers say "at company's discretion" or "subject to board approval."

This means they can deny your exercise → you lose everything.

Never sign an offer with an unapproved exercise window.

---

🚩 Red Flag #3: No cliff provision

4-year vesting with 1-year cliff is standard.

No cliff? You vest monthly starting Day 1.

Sounds generous, right?

Wrong. It removes leverage for both sides.
If you quit in month 3, you walk away with equity.
If they fire you in month 3, same thing.

Standard cliff protects BOTH parties.

---

🚩 Red Flag #4: "Broad-based" weighted average anti-dilution

This sounds investor-friendly.

But read closer: "broad-based" includes all outstanding shares.

"Narrow-based" only considers preferred shares.

Broad-based = better for employees.
Narrow-based = better for investors.

Most offers use narrow-based → silent dilution.

---

🚩 Red Flag #5: No 409A valuation disclosure

Your strike price should be based on a recent 409A valuation.

If the offer doesn't disclose it → you can't verify your discount.

Common at early-stage, but ask anyway:
- What was the last 409A?
- When was it done?
- How close are we to the next one?

## Thread 6: Startup Exit Calculator

1/6 Your startup equity is worth $100K on paper.

At exit, you get... $5K.

This happens ALL THE TIME.

Here's why:

---

📉 Scenario 1: Liquidation Preference

Investor put in $10M at 1x liquidation preference.

Company sells for $8M.

Investor gets $8M first (their preference).
Common stock (employees) gets $0.

Your $100K equity → $0.

This is why "participating preferred" is so dangerous.

Investors get their money back PLUS their pro-rata share of remaining proceeds.

Everyone else gets diluted twice.

---

📉 Scenario 2: Dilution from Future Rounds

You're 1% equity at Series A.

Series B: 20% dilution → 0.8%
Series C: 20% dilution → 0.64%
Series D: 20% dilution → 0.51%

Your $100K at Series A → $51K by Series D.

And that's BEFORE liquidation preference.

---

📉 Scenario 3: Early exercise vs. wait

Option grant: 10,000 shares at $1 strike.
Current 409A: $2/share.
Exit value: $10/share.

Exercise now: Pay $10K, get $100K = $90K profit.
Wait too long: Miss the window → $0.

Most employees don't realize their options have an expiration date.
