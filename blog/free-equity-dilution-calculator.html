What Is Equity Dilution?

Equity dilution happens when a company issues new shares, reducing the ownership percentage of existing shareholders. For founders, this typically occurs during:

  • Funding rounds — Investors buy equity, adding new shareholders
  • Option pool creation — Shares reserved for employees dilute existing owners
  • Convertible note conversion — SAFEs and notes convert to equity at a discount
  • Cofounder splits — Adding team members means dividing the pie
20-25%
Typical dilution per funding round (investor share)

Here's the math: if you own 50% of your company and raise a round where investors take 20%, your new ownership is 50% × (1 - 0.20) = 40%. But it's rarely that simple — investors often require an option pool expansion that dilutes you further.

Common Dilution Mistakes

After helping founders model their equity, here are the most common mistakes we see:

  1. Forgetting the option pool — Investors often require a 10-20% option pool. This dilutes you before the investment even happens.
  2. Underestimating total dilution — Founders often calculate one round but forget to model all future rounds. The compounding effect is significant.
  3. Ignoring SAFE conversion — SAFEs convert at a discount, which means more dilution than founders expect. Use our SAFE calculator to model this.
  4. Not benchmarking — Without comparing to industry averages, you can't tell if your terms are competitive.
  5. Over-optimizing for control — Some dilution is the cost of growth. The goal is smart dilution, not zero dilution.
