How Convertible Notes Work: Complete Guide with Calculator

What Is a Convertible Note?

A convertible note is a debt instrument that converts into equity at a future financing round. Think of it as a loan with an unusual repayment plan: instead of getting cash back, the investor receives shares.

Key characteristics:

  • It's debt initially: The note has a principal amount, interest rate, and maturity date
  • It converts later: At a qualified financing round (typically Series A), the note converts into equity
  • It has investor protections: Caps, discounts, and interest ensure investors get a fair deal

Why Use Convertible Notes?

Pros for Founders

  • Speed: Close funding in days, not months
  • Deferred valuation: Avoid contentious pricing discussions
  • Lower legal fees: Simpler than priced rounds
  • Flexibility: Can structure terms creatively

Cons for Founders

  • Dilution uncertainty: Won't know exact ownership until conversion
  • Debt overhang: Maturity date creates pressure
  • Complex math: Conversion mechanics get tricky
  • Cap stacking: Multiple notes can create problems

Key Terms Explained

Principal Amount

The cash amount invested. If an investor puts in $100,000, that's the principal. Interest accrues on this amount, increasing the total that converts.

Interest Rate

Convertible notes accrue interest (typically 4-8% annually), but this isn't paid in cash. Instead, it increases the principal amount that converts into equity.

Example: $100,000 principal at 6% annual interest converts to $106,000 after one year. The extra $6,000 buys more shares at the conversion price.

Valuation Cap

The cap is the maximum price per share the investor will pay. It protects the investor by ensuring they get a minimum percentage regardless of how high the valuation goes.

Conversion Price = min(Price at Qualified Financing, Valuation Cap / Fully Diluted Shares)
Example: You raise at a $20M valuation, but the note has a $8M cap. The investor's conversion price is based on $8M, not $20M. They get 2.5x more shares.

Discount

The discount (typically 15-25%) gives the investor a cheaper price per share than the new investors pay.

Conversion Price = Qualified Financing Price × (1 - Discount)
Example: New investors pay $2/share. With a 20% discount, note holders convert at $1.60/share.

Cap vs. Discount: Which Applies?

When both a cap and discount exist, the investor gets the lower conversion price. The conversion price is the lesser of:

  • Cap-based price
  • Discounted price

Maturity Date

The date by which the note must convert or be repaid. Typically 18-24 months. If no qualified financing occurs by maturity, the note converts at a preset price or must be repaid (though repayment is rare).

Conversion Mechanics: How It Actually Works

At a qualified financing round (usually $1M+), the note converts. Here's the step-by-step process:

  1. Calculate the conversion principal: Original amount + accrued interest
  2. Determine conversion price: The lower of cap-based or discount-based price
  3. Calculate shares issued: Conversion principal ÷ Conversion price
  4. Issue shares to note holder: These are added to the pre-money capitalization

The Conversion Formula

Shares to Note Holder = (Principal + Interest) ÷ Conversion Price

Where Conversion Price = min(
  Financing Round Price,
  Valuation Cap / Fully Diluted Shares,
  Financing Round Price × (1 - Discount)
)

Complete Example

Scenario:
• Note principal: $100,000
• Interest rate: 6% annually
• Valuation cap: $6M
• Discount: 20%
• Time to conversion: 1 year
• Series A valuation: $12M
• Series A price: $1.50/share
• Fully diluted shares: 8,000,000

Step 1: Calculate conversion principal
Principal = $100,000 + ($100,000 × 6%) = $106,000

Step 2: Determine conversion price
Cap price = $6,000,000 ÷ 8,000,000 = $0.75/share
Discount price = $1.50 × (1 - 20%) = $1.20/share
Conversion price = min($0.75, $1.20) = $0.75/share

Step 3: Calculate shares issued
Shares = $106,000 ÷ $0.75 = 141,333 shares

Result: The note holder receives 141,333 shares. At the Series A price of $1.50, these shares are worth $212,000—more than double their original investment.

Multiple Notes: The Stacking Problem

When you have multiple convertible notes with different caps and discounts, conversion gets complex. The key principles:

  • Each note converts separately: Calculate conversion for each note individually based on its own terms
  • Order matters for equity: Earlier investors typically get better terms (lower caps)
  • Total dilution adds up: All note holders receive shares before the Series A investment

Convertible Notes vs. SAFE

SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) are similar but key differences exist:

  • Debt vs. Equity: Notes are debt, SAFEs are not (no interest, no maturity)
  • Maturity: Notes have maturity dates; SAFEs don't expire
  • Complexity: SAFEs are simpler; notes have more terms
  • Legal precedent: Notes have established case law; SAFEs are newer

Negotiation Tips for Founders

Valuation Cap

  • Lower cap = more dilution: A $4M cap gives investors more shares than an $8M cap
  • Set realistic caps: Too low and you dilute excessively; too high and investors won't bite
  • Cap tiers: Consider different caps for different investors based on ticket size and timing

Discount

  • Standard is 15-25%: Anything higher is aggressive; anything lower limits investor interest
  • Discount vs. cap: The cap usually matters more in high-valuation scenarios
  • Pro-rata rights: Large note holders often ask for pro-rata rights in the next round

Interest Rate

  • 4-8% is standard: Higher rates increase dilution but are rarely the deciding factor
  • Negotiable but minor: Most variation happens in caps, not interest rates

Key Takeaways

  • Convertible notes are debt that converts to equity at a future financing round
  • Caps protect investors from overpaying; discounts reward early risk
  • Interest increases the conversion amount but isn't usually paid in cash
  • Conversion price is the lower of cap-based or discount-based price
  • Multiple notes with different terms require individual calculations
  • Model your conversion before signing to understand dilution impact
